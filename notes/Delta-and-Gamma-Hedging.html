    Delta and Gamma Hedging

    4 min read

    • #Economics/Finance
    • #task

    Motivation. Imagine you are an investment bank, selling (which is equivalent to short-selling) a call option. At the end, you have to pay to the holder CT​=max{ST​−K,0}. When the option is on the money at expiration time T, you have to pay ST​−K. You want to pay less, though; so you try to hedge against small changes in underlyer price.

    Delta Hedging for Call/Put Options §

    At time t=0 (day 0) §

    • Sell the option −CtE​, and get the cash CtE​
    • Borrow Lt​ amount of money to buy Δt​ units of stock. (this is the hedging part)
      • & this Δt​ is the same as the delta of the call option.
    Vt​= short-sell −CtE​​​+ cash CtE​​​− loan Lt​​​+ underlyer Δt​St​​​

    It is obvious that the value of this portfolio is Vt​=0, at time t=0, but to show that this portfolio at any time t always has value 0, we observe the fact that the last three terms (let Ctsyn​:=+CtE​−Lt​+Δt​St​) is a self-financing portflio that tracks the call price exactly. Observe from BSM derivation that a portfolio Wt​=nt​StC​+bt​Bt​ (we already used V) is self-financing and replicates when:

    • nt​=Stc​St​​Δf​
    • bt​=Bt​f(St​,t)−nt​StC​​ In our case, we have:
    • nt​=StC​St​​Δt​
    • bt​=Bt​CtE​−St​​ And thus =bt​Bt​CtE​−Lt​​​+=nt​St​Δt​St​​​ by the above equation.

    At time t=0+dt (day 1) §

    • Option value changes to −Ct+dt​
    • Cash holding grows to CtE​er⋅dt
    • We must “Re-hedge” or “balance” the loan and underlier such that we maintain Δt+dt​ units of underlyer. We break it out into three cases:
      • Δt+dt​=Δt​ (delta stays same): time value of money, and use dividend to pay back loan
        • Lt+dt​=Lt​ time value e(r−q)⋅dt​​
      • Δt​<Δt+dt​ (delta increases): Borrow more money to buy more stock:
        • Lt+dt​=Lt​e(r−q)dt+∣dΔt​∣St+dt​
      • Δt+dt​>Δt​ (delta decreases): Sell stock to reimburse loan.
        • Lt+dt​=Lt​e(r−q)dt−∣dΔt​∣St+dt​
      • In all three cases, we can say:
    Lt+dt​=Lt​e(r−q)dt+(dΔt​)St+dt​
    • We then purchase or sell dΔt​ units of the security to get Δt+dt​St+dt​ As we show above, Ct+dtsyn​:=+Ct+dtE​−Lt​+Δt+dt​St+dt​ is self-financing. This means that total value of the portfolio is:
    Vt+dt​​=−Ct+dtE​+CtE​er⋅dt−Lt+dt​=−Ct+dtE​+=Ct+dtE​CtE​er⋅dt−Lt​e(r−q)dt+(dΔt​)St+dt​​ loan ​+Δt+dt​St+dt​​​=0​​

    At time t=T, (expiration date) §

    On expiration and before the call is cashed out, the value of the portfolio is:

    VT​=0=−max{ST​−K,0}+C0E​erτ−LT​L0​e(r−q)τ+(dΔT−dt​)ST​​​+ΔT​ST​

    We then consider our customer cashing out their loan:

    1. If ST​>K in the money,
      1. −CTE​ becomes −ST​+K
      2. ΔT​=1
      3. Thus VT​=−ST​+K+C0E​erτ−LT​+ST​=0 since synthetic call still holds
        1. i.e., liquidate the stock to pay ST​
      4. Thus LT​=K+C0E​erτ
        1. i.e., pay back the loan with remaining K and cash invested
    2. If ST​≤K out of the money:
      1. −CtE​ becomes 0
      2. Thus VT​=+C0E​erτ−LT​+ΔT​ST​=0 since synthetic call still holds
      3. Thus LT​=C0E​erτ+ΔT​ST​
        1. i.e. pay back the loan with cash invested and liquidating the stock

    Greek Neutral Portfolio of Derivatives §

    Any derivative we can modify to make delta-neutral. For a security modeled by geometric brownian motion (with no dividends, q=0):

    dSt​=mSt​dt+σSt​dBt​

    Notation

    Since there are lots of notaions here, let’s outline them first:

    • St​ is the underlying security
    • CtE​ is the call option
    • V~ is the delta-neutral portfolio when parameter Ns​=ΔC​
    • V^ is the gamma-neutral portflio when parameters Ns​=ΔC​, NC​=−ΓC​Γ~​
    • V′ is the delta-gamma neutral portflio when parameters

    let us create a portfolio of a derivative of this stock by having one call option and Ns​ units of the underlier. The value of this portflio is:

    V~(St​,t)=CtE​+Ns​St​
    • CtE​ have greeks ΔC​,ΓC​,ΘC​
    • which have greeks Δ~=ΔC​+NS​, Γ~=ΓC​, Θ~=ΘC​. Using ito’s formula, we get:
    dV~​=​Θ~∂t∂V~​​​+mSt​Δ~∂St​∂V~​​​+21​σ2St2​Γ~∂St2​∂2V~​​​​dt+σSt​Δ~∂St​∂V~​​​dBt​=Γ~21​ See below σ2St2​dt​​+Θ~dt+Δ~(=dSt​mSt​dt+σSt​dBt​​​)=21​(dSt​)2Γ~+Θ~dt+Δ~dSt​​​

    Where from Ito Isometries

    (dS_{t})^{2}&=m^{2}S_{t}^{2}\underbrace{ (dt)^{2} }_{ =0 }+\sigma^{2}S_{t}^{2}\underbrace{ d\mathfrak{B}_{t}^{2} }_{ = dt}+2m\sigma S_{t}^{2}\underbrace{ dtd\mathfrak{B}_{t} }_{ =0 } \\ &= \sigma^{2}S_{t}^{2}dt \end{align}

    Now, from the above formula we see that there are three components that cause changes in dV~

    • ∣Δ~∣>0 i.e. non-zero delta
    • ∣Γ~∣>0, i.e. non-zero gamma
    • ∣Θ~∣>0, but considering Θ:=∂t∂S​ it’s impossible for this to be zero.

    Delta hedging. §

    Lets make Δ~=0. We can do this simply by setting Ns​≡ΔC​:

    V~∂St​∂V~​​=−CtE​+ΔS​St​=−∂St​∂V​+ΔC​=−ΔC​+ΔC​=0​​

    Delta-Gamma Hedging §

    After the delta hedging we still have a positive gamma. Lets make Γ~=0. We create a new portfolio of the delta-neutral portfolio, and NC​ units of the call:

    V^Γ^​=V~+NC​CtE​=∂St2​∂2V^​=Γ~+NC​ΓC​​​

    Setting NC​≡−ΓC​Γ~​ will make Γ^=0. But this incurs a new problem: Δ^ is non-zero again. We add Nmore​ more units of St​ to combat this:

    V′=V~+NC​CtE​+Nmore​
